Beer Growlers questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A beer growler is a container used to transport draft beer from a brewery or taproom. Glass growlers let you see the beer but are fragile, while stainless steel growlers are durable and often insulated to keep beer cold and carbonated longer. Your choice depends on how far you travel, how long you plan to store the beer, and your budget.

What is a beer growler and how does it work?

A beer growler is a jug or bottle designed to carry fresh draft beer from a brewery or bar to your home. The classic version is a half-gallon glass jug with a screw-top or swing-top lid. Bartenders fill it straight from the tap, seal it, and you carry it out the door. The name supposedly comes from the sound beer made sloshing inside old metal pails as workers carried them home. The key to a growler’s function is a tight seal. Without it, carbonation escapes and beer goes flat within hours. Most growlers rely on either a threaded cap with a rubber gasket, a swing-top with a rubber seal, or a counter-pressure fill system that maintains CO2. Modern stainless steel growlers often add vacuum insulation to slow temperature changes, which helps preserve carbonation longer.

Glass vs stainless steel growlers: which is better?

Glass growlers are the traditional choice. They&#8217;re affordable, you can see the beer inside, and they don&#8217;t add any metallic taste. But glass is fragile, it breaks easily if dropped or bumped, and it&#8217;s heavy when full. Glass also lacks insulation, so the beer warms up quickly unless you keep it in a cooler. And because glass is transparent, light can skunk hoppy beers if you leave them in the sun. Stainless steel growlers are tougher and often double-walled, keeping beer cold for hours even without a cooler. They block all light, protecting the beer&#8217;s flavor. Many models are also vacuum-insulated, which helps keep carbonation stable for a day or two. Downsides: you can&#8217;t see how much beer is left, and quality-insulated steel growlers cost significantly more. They&#8217;re also heavier than glass when empty, though the weight trade-off often pays off if you&#8217;re hiking or biking to a brewery.

How to clean and maintain your growler properly

A clean growler is essential for good-tasting beer. Leftover residue can host bacteria or mold, and leftover soap can kill foam. Rinse your growler with hot water as soon as it&#8217;s empty. For a deeper clean, use a dedicated bottle brush and a mild, unscented cleaner like PBW or oxyclean-free solution. Avoid dish soap if possible, it can leave a film that ruins head retention. Rinse thoroughly with very hot water until no scent remains. Never put a glass growler in the dishwasher unless the manufacturer explicitly says it&#8217;s safe. The high heat and harsh detergent can weaken the glass or damage the seal. Stainless steel growlers can often go in the dishwasher, but check the care instructions first; the exterior finish or insulation may suffer. After cleaning, let the growler air-dry upside down on a rack. Store it with the lid off to prevent trapped moisture and smells.

Do growlers keep beer carbonated? How long is beer fresh?

A properly sealed growler will keep beer carbonated for about 24 to 48 hours if kept cold. After that, the carbon dioxide slowly leaks out through the seal or the cap, and the beer begins to go flat. Glass growlers with a simple screw cap lose carbonation faster than a well-sealed swing-top or a counter-pressure fill. Insulated stainless steel growlers help by keeping the beer cold, which slows gas escape, but even they are not airtight forever. For the freshest taste, drink your growler the same day you buy it. By day two you may notice some loss of bubbles and a slightly muted aroma. By day three the beer is often noticeably flat and oxidized. A few specialty breweries use &#8220;growler fills&#8221; that purge the container with CO2 before filling, which can extend freshness to three or four days. But as a rule of thumb, treat your growler like a time-sensitive treat: fill it, chill it, drink it fast.

What sizes and cap types are available?

The classic growler size is 64 ounces (a half-gallon), but you&#8217;ll also find 32-ounce &#8220;howlers&#8221; and even 128-ounce jugs. Smaller sizes are easier to carry and finish before the beer goes flat. Larger sizes are better for parties but put more pressure on the seal and on your arm. Many breweries standardize on one size to simplify filling, so check ahead if you&#8217;re buying a growler from a specific taproom. Cap types matter a lot. The simplest is a threaded plastic or metal cap with a rubber gasket inside. These work fine for a day but can be tricky to tighten evenly. Swing-top (flip-top) caps with a rubber gasket and wire clamp give a tighter, more consistent seal and are easier to open. Some stainless steel growlers use a twist-off cap with an O-ring seal or a pressure-release valve for fills under CO2. Avoid cheap corks or stoppers, they don&#8217;t seal well and can let carbonation escape quickly.

How to choose the right growler for your needs

Start with your typical use. If you mostly visit a brewery a few blocks away and drink the beer that evening, a simple glass 64-ounce screw-top is perfectly fine and budget-friendly. If you often travel longer distances or bike to a taproom, a stainless steel insulated growler is worth the investment, it keeps beer cold for hours and won&#8217;t shatter in your backpack. Consider the brewery&#8217;s fill method too. Some breweries only fill certain types of growlers because of their seal design. If you plan to collect growlers from different places, choose a style with a standard 2-inch cap opening (most breweries can fill those). For homebrewers who want to bottle carbonated beer, swing-top glass growlers are reliable and easy to clean. And if you care about sustainability, a durable stainless steel growler will last for years, while glass may need replacing if you drop it.

Frequently asked questions

Can you put hot liquid in a glass growler?

Never pour hot coffee, tea, or boiling water into a glass growler. Most glass growlers are not tempered for heat and can shatter from thermal shock. Stick to cold beverages only. Some stainless steel growlers are designed for hot drinks, but always check the label.

Are stainless steel growlers dishwasher safe?

Many stainless steel growlers are top-rack dishwasher safe, but the intense heat can damage the vacuum seal over time. Hand-washing with warm water and a mild cleaner is safer for the insulation. Always consult the manufacturer&#8217;s instructions before running one through the dishwasher.

Do growlers need to be pressurized?

No, a standard growler fill does not require external pressure. The beer is carbonated already, and a tight seal traps that carbonation. Some advanced &#8220;pressurized growlers&#8221; have a built-in CO2 cartridge to keep beer fresh for weeks, but they are a separate product category called &#8220;counter-pressure growlers.&#8221; For daily use, a simple seal is enough.

How should you store a filled growler?

Store a filled growler upright in your refrigerator as soon as possible. The cold temperature slows carbonation loss and prevents bacterial growth. Keep it away from direct light and strong odors. If you have a glass growler, don&#8217;t store it on its side, the beer can leak around the seal.

Can you use a growler for homebrew?

Yes, glass swing-top growlers are popular for bottling homebrew because they seal tightly and are easy to clean. However, you must use the proper amount of priming sugar and be careful not to over-carbonate. Glass growlers are not pressure-rated like beer bottles, so they can explode if you add too much sugar. Stick to styles with low carbonation and leave headspace.

What&#8217;s the difference between a growler and a crowler?

A crowler is a 32-ounce aluminum can that is filled and seamed at the brewery, similar to a beer can. It is single-use and recyclable, while a growler is a reusable container. Crowlers are more portable and seal at a higher carbonation level, making them last longer than a typical glass growler fill, often up to a week unopened.

In shortThe right growler depends on how you drink and how far you carry it. Glass is classic and cheap, but fragile; stainless steel is tough and insulated, but pricier. Whichever you choose, keep it clean, keep it cold, and drink your beer fresh. For most people, starting with a simple 64-ounce swing-top glass growler is the easiest way to enjoy draft beer at home without a big investment.
